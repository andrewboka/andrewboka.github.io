Entry 2

Raj Chetty's work is quite extensive and I spent a majority of this week reading through his various papers and trying to understand them better. Since he releases his data for public use, I plan to create a simple visualization of intergenerational mobility in different areas of the United States.

The first paper I read dealt with the geography of intergenerational mobility in the United States. In the paper, two measures of economic mobility were explored: relative mobility and absolute upward mobility. The former is a measure in the difference between expected economic outcomes between children from high-income and low-income families. Visually, this is represented as the slope on a graph plotting parent income rank against mean child income rank. The latter measures the expected economic outcomes of children born to a family in the 25th percentile of the income distribution. Absolute upward mobility is the main measurement Chetty uses to compare the intergenerational mobility in different states. Chetty recognized that much of the variation in children's outcomes emerges before they enter the labor market which suggests that the differences in mobility are driven by factors that affect children while they are growing up. Ultimately, he identified five major factors that are strongly correlated with mobility: 1) segreation; 2) inequality; 3) school quality; 4) social capital indices; and 5) family structure. I think it would be interesting to explore these areas for two different major cities that lie at opposite ends of the mobility spectrum.

Chetty also explores trends in mobility since the 1940s and shows that the fraction of children earning more than their parents has fallen from 90% in 1940 to about 50% for children born today.

Entry 6

I decided that I needed to review papers that explored the topic of family complexity so that I could perform a more accurate analysis of the data. The main study I referenced was titled Family Complexity among Children in the United States . This study used the Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP) dataset to measure family complexity. Family structure only encapsulates the biological relationship of parents to a child along with the marital or union status of parents. However, there are more complex relationships within families, such as sibling relationships, that are not measured by family structure alone. Family complexity can be decomposed into two things: family structure (parental relationshipst o children and one another) and sibling composition (half, step, full siblings). They classify children living in complex families as those who reside with a single parent, stepparent, half sibling or stepsibling. Complex families can have either parent complexity, sibling complexity or both. Previous studies have shown that children raised with a stepparent experience more negative outcomes than children raised by two biological parents. Similarly, children raised by two cohabiting biological parents do not fare as well as those raised by married biological parents. The study also found a relationship between higher complexity levels and lower education levels. Due to these findings, I expect to find a similar relationship between higher levels of parent complexity and lower levels of economic mobility.


Entry 7

The study discussed in the previous journal entry used the SIPP dataset to gather data about parent complexity. I decided that I should use the data from the 2014 SIPP to measure levels of family complexity in different states. The SIPP is a longitudinal household survey conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau. The survey's mission is to provide a nationally representative sample for evaluating: 1) annual and sub-annual income dynamics; 2) movements into and out of government transfer programs; 3) family and social context of individuals and households; and 4) interactins among these items. The 2014 SIPP was administed in panels and conuducted in waves. For the purposes of this study, I simply used Wave 1 data which represents the data gathered from the interviewer the first time they contact a household. Each person is uniquely identified by a combination of their household number and person number within that household. The relationships between individuals within a household are also characterized by a relationship matrix where the diagonal consists of individuals relationships to themselves and all other entries consist of the relationship of the row person number to the column person number. This will allow me to build a comprehensive overview of each household and identify the complexity within each household.

As a sanity check, the first graph I created described the relationship between simple two parent biological families and mobility rank. The relationship was positive (the higher the proportion of simple two parent biological families, the higher the mobility rank) and also significant (p~0.04). The next graph described the relationship between parent complexity and mobility rank, which was negative (higher parent complexity implies a lower mobility rank) and significant as well (p~0.004). The third graph used showed the relationship between sibling complexity and mobility rank which was also negative (higher sibling complexity implies lower mobility rank) and significant (p~0.01). The final graph showed the relationship between family complexity (both parent and sibling complexity) which was also negative and significant (p~0.002). Interestingly, while all results are significant it is clear that using a measure of parent complexity, sibling complexity or family complexity all produce results with a significantly lower p-value and thus there is a much lower probability of obtaining the observed results given that the null hypothesis (no relationship) is true.
